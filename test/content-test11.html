Assange is no hero, but he deserves better from our govt

Australian leaders like to claim, disingenuously, that a characteristic of the alliance with America is that Australia reserves the right to object, in a friendly if forceful way, with US policy or decisions.

No such objections have ever been publically expressed. It is time to test it.

An Australian citizen is in real trouble abroad. Julian Assange faces decades in prison in the US, which has an atrocious record for grabbing foreign suspects and holding them long years without trial.

That Assange mistakes himself for an oracle when he’s just a rooster is irrelevant. Australia’s lack of support for him is total. It carries hints of payback and moralistic exulting.

Assange believes he’s been abandoned by Australia, which says it has no place to interfere in extradition hearings between other countries.

That is no doubt legally correct, but the Australian spokespeople on this matter, Julia Gillard, Bob Carr and Nicola Roxon, know Assange is facing the most ruthless prosecutorial office on earth: the US District Attorney, likely overseen in this case by its Southern District office in Manhattan.

This is the group that convenes grand juries in the absence of the suspect, parades unchallenged “evidence” before jurors, always wins indictments, and then sends its agents abroad to drag home Colombian drug lords or 9/11 terror suspects such as Khalid Shiekh Mohammad.

The US ranks Assange in this class. Waterboarding is nowadays off the menu in the US, but a foreigner such as Assange could easily disappear into a federal prison for years.

This is not the kind of justice that Australia, let alone America, should support.

Assange is holed up in the Ecuador Embassy in London, waiting to learn whether it will grant him asylum in order to avoid extradition to Sweden to face the rape allegations that emerged so uncannily and fortuitously for Assange’s enemies.

British police will arrest him the moment he leaves the embassy, meaning his immunity campout is no more than an attempt to buy time.

Assange fears the fix is in: once in Sweden, whether he is tried and found guilty of the sex crimes or not, the US will grab him to face trial under the 1917 Espionage Act for the mass WikiLeaks releases.

Britain, at this time, is following the orders of its own courts by agreeing to extradite Assange to Sweden. But it appears to regard the US complaint very differently.

Britain, also a victim of WikiLeaks, values its international standing too highly to be seen as a pawn of America, especially in what is clearly a free speech case; and the British public, whether they like Assange or not, would regard his forcible removal to the US as a dangerous attack on their sovereignty.

Assange fears Sweden is a mere stopover on his way to the US. It is widely believed that federal prosecutors in America have already held a secret grand jury and indicted him.

Julia Gillard has been careful to focus on the Swedish case, treating him as a sex suspect. Her government claims no knowledge of the US indictment. Bob Carr seems to think, wrongly, that the Swedish extradition might as well go ahead because the US would win an extradition application from Britain anyway.

Clearly, Australia cannot intervene in the Swedish extradition process now that the British courts have ruled it is legal. But it can insist that Sweden not extradite Assange to the US to face trial. It should also find courage to oppose the American pressganging of an Australian.

Assange knowingly distributed classified US material, some of which was published in the New York Times, and elsewhere. WikiLeaks was fascinating – especially the Moscow embassy staffer’s account of the Caucasus wedding. We all skimmed it. We are all complicit.

A question at a US trial would be whether Assange, as a blogger, enjoys the same First Amendment free speech rights of media organisations, which have not been prosecuted for reprinting the same material.

Assange, as a non-citizen, may not even be able to invoke First Amendment rights. But his biggest problem is that he is not a loveable character who would come off badly before an American jury.

The Americans have already got their man – Private Bradley Manning, the Iraq soldier who allegedly leaked the material. They don’t need Assange.

Australia’s silence diminishes us all. Whether Assange belongs in a Swedish prison remains to be seen, but he does not belong with America’s languishing drug lords and terrorists.

Paul Toohey is News Ltd’s US Correspondent. tooheyp@newsltd.com.au
