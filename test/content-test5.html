A rough guide to refuge in Ecuador

June 21, 2012 - 12:42PM

Opinion

  • Be the first to comment

So Julian Assange wants to become an Ecuadorean. Nothing wrong with that. An asylum seeker no less, though all he had to do was walk into Ecuador’s embassy in London and ask for it.

It’s not certain how long he may have to stay in the embassy before his application for political asylum in Ecuador is processed, though the country's government says there may be a decision as early as tomorrow.

The most extreme case of someone living in an embassy is that of Cardinal Jozsef Mindszenty, who sought refuge in the US embassy in Budapest in 1956. Mindszenty, who had opposed communism, was freed from jail during the Hungarian uprising, but sought asylum when the Soviet tanks rolled in to crush the dissent. He stayed 15 years.

Assange and his supporters say he was driven to his action after he lost his last avenue of appeal in fighting extradition to Sweden to face questioning over sex allegations.

From there, he believed it would be only a small step to his extradition to the US, which, his supporters say, wants to jail him and throw away the key over WikiLeaks’ disclosures.

But it has all become rather messy because if he leaves the embassy, British police officers will arrest him for breaching his bail conditions, even if he has been granted asylum or been given a diplomatic passport.

The irony here is that if he were to be given Ecuadorean nationality he couldn’t then take refuge again in the embassy. Getting to an airport is going to be rather problematic. A blanket over the head, spirited away in the back seat of an undetected car and then a boat to his new home, would that be the future?

He may take comfort in knowing the latter is how many asylum seekers look to the future. Still, Ecuador would find Britain rather displeased if that were to happen.

Assange does, however, have a friend in Ecuador’s President Rafael Correa — the President appeared as a guest on Assange’s TV chat show and he booted out the US ambassador after a WikiLeaks cable revealed purported US influence in the police force.

Given the wheels of diplomacy can be unpredictable, Assange would probably have time enough at the embassy to immerse himself in the details of his possible new country. He could visit the CIA World Factbook for the information — another irony.

For instance, there he would find that the climate is variable: tropical in parts, cooler inland. Indeed, some travel agents describe Ecuador as experiencing four seasons in a day. Being a part-Melbourne boy, he’d feel at home with that.

The equator runs through the country, which is bordered by the Pacific, Peru and Colombia.

It is ‘‘slightly smaller than Nevada’’, including the Galapagos Islands, if that’s any help.

Its official language is Spanish. Its dominant religion is Roman Catholic (95 per cent of the population), so you could expect a fair amount of people praying for Assange, one way or another.

Its natural resources are fish, timber, petroleum and hydropower. Its natural hazards include ‘‘frequent earthquakes, landslides, volcanic activity, floods and periodic droughts’’. This is not the benign English countryside, where he has been living recently.

Two-thirds of the country’s 15 million people live in cities. Life expectancy runs at about 75 years. Just over a quarter of the population live below the poverty line.

Despite President Correa taking swings against American imperialism, the US is a major trading partner, accounting for 45 per cent of exports and 25 per cent of imports.

Importantly, for Assange, Ecuador has more than 160,000 internet hosts. It also has 105 airports with paved runways and 323 with unpaved runways — and two heliports. It has a coastline of 2237 kilometres.

Assange, if he makes it, will find he is not the only asylum seeker in Ecuador. The CIA Factbook cites UN refugee agency figures showing more than 11,000 refugees from Colombia in Ecuador and a possible 250,000 seeking asylum because of the drug-related violence in their own country.

Assange may become one among many; though one who, unlike his fellow refugees, possesses one of the most recognisable faces in the world.
